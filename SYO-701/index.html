    Confidentiality

    Protect the information from unauthorized access and disclosure (Could be an individual, entity or process).

    Keeping information secret and safe from prying eyes, only access who they need to access.

    2. Maintain a business advantage
    3. Achieve regulatory compliance (PII, PHI, Various types of financial data

    1. Encryption
    2. Access Controls
    3. Data Masking
    4. Physical Security Measures
    5. Training and awareness

    üì∂ Availability Overview

    Ensures that information, systems, and resources are accessible and operational when needed by authorized users. | Employees can access internal systems 24/7 without downtime. |

    üîé Why Availability Matters

    Benefit Description Example
    Business Continuity Keeps operations running during failures A backup server activates instantly after the main one fails.
    Customer Trust Maintains reliable services Users trust online banking due to constant service uptime.
    Organization's Reputation Avoids negative impact due to outages A retail site remains online during high-traffic holiday sales.

    üîÅ Redundancy Strategies:

    To overcome the challenges associated with maintaining availability, the best strategy is to use redundancy in the systems and network designs.

    Redundancy Type Description Example
    Redundancy Duplication of critical components or functions to enhance reliability. Having a backup internet connection and server for critical services.
    Server Redundancy Uses multiple servers in a load-balanced or failover configuration to maintain service availability. If one server crashes, others continue handling user traffic.
    Data Redundancy Stores data in multiple locations to prevent loss or access failure. Cloud storage syncing data across different regions.
    Network Redundancy Uses multiple network paths to ensure data delivery if one route fails. Internet reroutes through a backup ISP line if the main one is down.
    Power Redundancy Uses backup power sources like UPS or generators to avoid power-related downtime. Data center switches to generator during a city-wide blackout.

    Something You Do (Action) Depends on a user‚Äôs unique behavior or action. Typing rhythm, voice recognition.
    Somewhere You Are (Location) Uses the user's geographic location as a factor in authentication. Logging in only when located at the office or within a country.

    üîê Zero Trust: Never trust, always verify...

    It assumes that threats can come from inside or outside the network and that no user or device should be automatically trusted‚Äîeven if it's inside the perimeter.

    This mean we can not just trust to the network perimeter to protect the system as a main line of defense, with deperimeterization (‚ÄúDon‚Äôt protect just the perimeter ‚Äî protect everything, everywhere.‚Äù)we are put a lot or risk into the organization that is why we need to focus on: strong authentication, data encryption access controls per user/devices, zero trust architecture.

    Deperimeterization is removing the traditional network boundary as the main line of defense. Instead of relying only on firewalls or network borders, security is applied directly to data, users, and devices ‚Äî no matter where they are.

    Types Objective Description
    Control Plane is the brain (making decisions) Adaptive Identity, threat scope reduction, policy-driven access control, secured zones with Policy Engine and Policy Administrator.
    Data Plane is the hands (doing the work) Subject/System and Policy Enforcement Point

    üìå Control Plane

    Control Plane Description Example
    Control Plane responsible for defining, managing, and enforcing the policies related to user and system access.
    Adaptive Identity validation into account user behavior, device, location and more. üîí Example: If you usually log in from:A laptop in New York at 9 AM But suddenly: There‚Äôs a login attempt from Brazil at 3 AM on a phone ‚û°Ô∏è The system might block the login or ask for extra verification (like a code).
    Threat scope reduction Limit he access only for the need work tasks, avoid risk and compromise credentials. A customer support agent: ‚úÖ Can access customer tickets ‚ùå Cannot access payment systems or internal code. So if their account is compromised, critical systems stay safe.
    Policy-driven access control Enforce user access policies based on their roles and responsibilities. Instead of manually assigning access, the system checks a policy like:‚ÄúAllow access if the user is in the HR department and using a company device.‚Äù
    Secured zones Isolated environments within a network that are designed to house sensitive data.They are like "digital vaults" ‚Äî only trusted users and systems can enter, and even then, with strict controls. ‚úÖ Example:A company‚Äôs HR database lives in a Secured Zone. Only HR personnel with verified devices can access it, and only from inside the corporate VPN.using firewalls, access controls, and micro-segmentation

    Control Plane uses: Policy Engine and Policy Administrator to makes security decisions.

    Decision-making and enforcement What it does How it works Example Question?
    Policy Engine Makes decisions based on policies: allow or block. Evaluates context like user identity, device health, location, and request type. Decides if a user with a healthy laptop and MFA can access a sensitive app. Should this request be allowed?
    Policy Administrator Enforces the decision from the Policy Engine. Connects to the Policy Enforcement Point (PEP) to allow or block traffic. Sends an ‚Äúallow‚Äù command to the firewall or gateway to permit access. Apply the Policy Engine‚Äôs decision

    The Policy Enforcement Point (PEP) is the security gateway or system that executes the decision: allow, block, or limit user/device access.

    üîÅ Flow Summary:
    1. User makes a request.
    2. Policy Engine evaluates based on policies
    3. Policy Administrator applies the decision via the Policy Enforcement Point (PEP).

    üìå Data Plane

    üß± Core Principles of Zero Trust

    Principle Description
    Verify Explicitly Authenticate and authorize based on identity, location, device health, etc.
    Least Privilege Access Limit access strictly to what is needed for a role.
    Assume Breach Design with the mindset that a breach has or could happen.
    Micro-Segmentation Split networks into smaller zones to isolate access.
    Continuous Monitoring Use real-time data to detect and respond to threats.

    üõ†Ô∏è Key Components

    Network Segmentation Isolate parts of the network to reduce attack spread.
    Data Protection Encrypt data and control who can access it.
    Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Monitor and analyze security events.

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Gap Analysis

    Gap Analysis = Finding the "Difference" between where you are and where you want to be.

    Is the process used to compare the current state of a process, system, or performance against a desired future state. It helps identify the "gaps" or differences that need to be addressed to achieve the goals.

    Example:
    A company wants to improve its cybersecurity posture. The current state shows basic firewall protection, but the desired state includes advanced threat detection and response capabilities.
    The gap analysis reveals missing tools and processes that must be implemented to reach the desired security level.

    Current State Desired State Identified Gap
    Basic firewall protection Advanced threat detection & response Lack of security monitoring tools
    Manual patch management Automated patch deployment Need for automation in updates
    Limited employee training Regular security awareness training Insufficient user education

    üïµÔ∏è‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Threat Actor: Motivation vs. Intent

    Understanding the difference between motivation and intent helps in identifying the purpose and target of a cyberattack.

    Term Definition Question it Answers Example
    Motivation The real reason or personal drive behind the attacker‚Äôs actions. Why is the attacker doing this? Anger, money, politics, curiosity, revenge, ideology.
    Intent The specific goal the attacker wants to achieve with the attack. What does the attacker want to achieve? Stealing data, damaging systems, stopping services, spying.

    üîÅ Analogy

    üõ¢Ô∏è Motivation is the fuel in the car ‚Äî it powers the attacker.
    üéØ Intent is the destination ‚Äî it‚Äôs what the attacker wants to reach.

    üß® Real-World Example

    • A hacktivist‚Äôs motivation is a strong political belief (anti-censorship).
    • Their intent is to deface a government website to send a message.

    Understanding both helps in predicting, preventing, and responding to threats more effectively.

    üéØ Threat Actor Motivations

    Concept Description Example Real Case Example
    Data Exfiltration Stealing sensitive data from a system or organization. A hacker extracts customer data from a retail company's database. Equifax breach (2017) ‚Äì Sensitive data of 147 million people was stolen.
    Blackmail Using stolen or sensitive information to coerce or threaten someone. An attacker threatens to leak private employee emails unless paid. Ashley Madison hack (2015) ‚Äì Attackers threatened to leak user info.
    Espionage Spying to gain confidential information, often for competitive advantage. A state-sponsored group steals military research from another country. APT10 (China) ‚Äì Stole IP from Western companies and governments.
    Service Disruption Interrupting services to cause downtime or operational chaos. A DDoS attack takes down a hospital‚Äôs online appointment system. Dyn DDoS attack (2016) ‚Äì Disrupted Spotify, Twitter, Netflix, etc.
    Financial Gain Committing cybercrimes for profit through theft or extortion. Ransomware locks files and demands payment to unlock them. WannaCry (2017) ‚Äì Ransomware attack affecting 200,000+ systems.
    Philosophical/Political Beliefs Hacking to support a cause or protest government or corporate actions. Hacktivists deface a government website to protest censorship. Anonymous vs. Scientology (2008) ‚Äì DDoS and defacements in protest.
    Ethical Reasons White-hat hacking to expose flaws and improve security. A security researcher responsibly reports a vulnerability to a vendor. Google Project Zero ‚Äì Reports zero-day vulnerabilities responsibly.
    Revenge Attacking to harm a person or organization out of anger. A fired employee deletes files or leaks company data. Tesla insider (2020) ‚Äì Tried to sabotage the company after being dismissed.
    Disruption/Chaos Causing damage or confusion with no clear goal, just to create chaos. Random malware spreads and crashes thousands of computers globally. NotPetya (2017) ‚Äì Caused global damage with no clear target.
    War (Cyber Warfare) Nation-state attacks as part of military or geopolitical conflict. A government disables another country‚Äôs power grid through malware. Stuxnet (2010) ‚Äì Malware targeting Iran's nuclear program.
